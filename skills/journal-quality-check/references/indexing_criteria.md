# Indexing criteria reference: Redalyc/AmeliCA, SciELO, Scopus, Web of Science, PubMed/MEDLINE

Sources: the Redalyc, Scopus, and Web of Science sections come from the ABEC Brasil minicurso "Indexação de Periódicos" (Luisa Veras de Sandes-Guimarães, UnB/FEA-USP), Parte 2 — treat the specific figures there (journal counts, acceptance rates) as snapshots from when those slides were produced. The SciELO section is drawn from the official "Critérios SciELO Brasil" document (September 2022 version); SciELO updates this periodically, so verify the current version on scielo.org for anything precision-sensitive. The PubMed/MEDLINE section is drawn directly from NLM's own current documentation (last reviewed September 2025) — this process changed recently (see that section) and may change again, so it's worth re-checking nlm.nih.gov/medline before relying on procedural specifics. Read `SKILL.md` first for when and how to use this file.

## Redalyc

Created 2003 (Universidad Autónoma del Estado de México). Focus: Latin America, Caribbean, Spain, Portugal, or journals elsewhere that publish on Latin America. Non-profit, diamond open-access model. ~1,368 journals, 679k articles, 26 countries (2020 figures — treat as historical, verify current scale if it matters for the user's question).

Evaluation has three blocks. A journal must satisfy ALL of Block 1, and at least 70% combined of Blocks 2+3 (minimum 32 of 45 items) to be accepted.

### Block 1 — Critérios Básicos de Admissão (CBA), 9 items, all mandatory
Failing any one of these can mean a 6-month embargo before resubmission.
1. **Antiguidade** — at least 2 years of existence, editions online.
2. **Revisão por pares** — all originals go through peer review; site must detail review type (anonymized or not, single/double-blind/open) and how disputes are resolved.
3. **Conteúdo científico** — at least 75% of each edition's contributions must be original research results (or other significant original contributions to the field).
4. **Exogeneidade na publicação** — at least 70% of published works per edition from contributors outside the publishing institution (minimum: at least one author outside the institution).
5. **Cumprimento da periodicidade** — editions published on time per the declared frequency; continuous-publication journals are marked down for gaps >3 months without new articles online.
6. **Não cobrar APC** — no fee charged to authors/institutions for evaluation or processing.
7. **XML JATS** — articles marked up in XML JATS (Redalyc provides the "Marcalyc" tool for this).
8. **Dados de identificação** — full title, ISSN (print and/or electronic), publishing institution.
9. **Política de acesso aberto** — must permit reading, download, copying, distribution, printing, search, and linking of full texts.

### Block 2 — Critérios Altamente Valorizados (CAV), Block 3 — Critérios Desejáveis (CD) — 38 + 7 = 45 items
★ marks a Critério Desejável (CD); unmarked items are CAV.

**Conteúdo Científico**
- Exogeneidade de avaliadores e do conselho/corpo editorial (external reviewers; 75% of editorial board external to the publishing institution)
- Articles stated as original and unpublished elsewhere
- Estimated evaluation/publication time stated
- ★ Publish the rejection rate annually
- Make the evaluation form available
- State submission and acceptance dates
- Do not publish issues with no scientific content

**Periodicidade**
- Specify the journal's frequency (monthly, bimonthly, etc., or continuous) and which months/day each edition covers
- ★ Specify the exact day editions are published

**Acesso Aberto**
- Explicitly state funding sources on the homepage
- State the license (Redalyc recommends CC BY-NC) on every article, all formats
- State who holds exploitation/patrimonial rights (author(s) or journal/publisher)
- State a self-archiving policy (when/which version authors may deposit)

**Gestão Editorial**
- Contact details of the publishing institution
- Declared scope, objective, and target audience
- Topical specificity (a defined focus, not a generic catch-all)
- Full editorial board roster: full names, full institutional affiliation with country (no abbreviations)
- ★ Bibliographic note on every article (journal name/ISSN, volume/issue, period covered, author names)
- Table of contents translated into a second language
- Author data: full name, institutional affiliation, country, email (≥1 author), no abbreviations
- Publish at least 16 articles/year
- Homogeneous article count across issues
- State current indexing (directories, databases, indices, with direct links)
- ★ Detail the citation/reference style used
- Abstract and keywords in two languages
- ★ Author instructions in two languages
- ★ Code of ethics (reference COPE/CSE or similar; state unacceptable practices and consequences)
- ★ Plagiarism/similarity detection method detailed
- Digital preservation policy (LOCKSS, CLOCKSS, Portico)

**Aproveitamento de Tecnologias**
- Independent journal website (not the same as the institution's)
- More than one download format (PDF, ePub, HTML)
- Electronic editorial management tool (e.g., OJS)
- Interoperability protocols (OAI-PMH)
- Search engine for the journal's content
- Individual article download
- All digital editions on a single site
- Request ORCID (or Redalyc ID) from authors
- Efficient navigability (content reachable in ≤3 clicks)
- Added value to users (multimedia, raw data access, accessibility services, promotion on Scholar/Mendeley/ResearchGate)
- User interaction (blogs, forums)
- Site/article usage statistics

### AmeliCA
A separate but linked infrastructure (UNESCO/CLACSO/Redalyc) focused on journals still consolidating. To apply: no APC, peer review in place, transitioning to XML-marked digital publication. Requests go to ameli.conocimientoabierto@gmail.com via the "Solicitud de ingreso a Redalyc y/o AmeliCA" form.

### Practical note
Regional/local journals are accepted, but in smaller proportion — mainly when they expand the indexer's coverage or bring a new perspective/specialty not yet represented. Being regional in these indices tends to correlate with lower impact indicators (smaller citation pools), which isn't itself a quality judgment.

## SciELO

A network of national/thematic open-access collections (Brazil, plus Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Cuba, Ecuador, Mexico, Paraguay, Peru, Portugal, South Africa, Spain, Uruguay, Venezuela, West Indies, and others), operated under the SciELO Program (FAPESP-supported, with CAPES, CNPq, and ABEC Brasil as supporting institutions for the Brazil collection). Each national/thematic collection has its own advisory committee and can adapt criteria to local conditions, but the Brazil collection's criteria document ("Critérios SciELO Brasil") serves as the reference for the network. **The detail below reflects the September 2022 version of Critérios SciELO Brasil — SciELO updates this document periodically (a Portugal-specific update exists from February 2025), so verify the current version on scielo.org if precision matters.**

### Governance
Admission and continued indexing decisions are made exclusively by the collection's **Comitê Consultivo** (Advisory Committee) — for Brazil, six elected chief editors representing major fields (Agricultural; Biological; Physical sciences, including Engineering and Earth Sciences; Humanities and Applied Social Sciences; Linguistics/Literature/Arts; Health), plus representatives of ABEC Brasil, CNPq, CAPES, and the SciELO Program director. Meets at least three times a year. Evaluation happens in two stages: a **pre-evaluation** (data collection against the criteria below) and a **merit evaluation** (scientific merit review by ad hoc referees in the journal's field). Outcomes range from immediate unrestricted entry to entry conditioned on fixing specific issues by a deadline, to rejection. Continued indexing is reassessed semi-annually for some criteria and annually for all of them.

### Key admission/permanence criteria (selective highlights — see the full document for everything)
- **Minimum existence**: at least 4 published issues (or the equivalent in continuous-publication articles).
- **Scientific character**: predominantly original research articles (reviews, data articles, and relevant essays also count); journal must be progressively aligned with open science practices (accepting preprint-deposited manuscripts, requiring data citation/availability statements, offering open peer review options) — full alignment was required by end of 2023.
- **No duplicate publication**: articles can't be republished or translated from elsewhere, though Creative-Commons-licensed derivative works with their own DOI are acceptable.
- **Editorial board**: defined chief editor(s) with public CV/ORCID; for the associate/section editor body, **at least 25% (recommended 35%) must hold a foreign institutional affiliation**; **gender composition must be at least 25% men and at least 25% women** among the editorial body (with a gender-equity policy required by end of 2024).
- **Author affiliation diversity**: full institutional + geographic affiliation required for every author, with ORCID iD. **Journals whose author affiliations are restricted to a single institution or geographic area are not considered for evaluation at all** — this is a direct anti-insularity rule.
- **Manuscript review**: must use a SciELO-certified online manuscript management system with specific features (ORCID-authenticated author registration, plagiarism-check interoperability, full process tracking, etc.). **Average time from submission to final decision: max 6 months; submission to publication: max 12 months** (6-month full cycle recommended). Continuous manuscript submission required — can't suspend intake. **International participation**: at least 15% (recommended 25%) of manuscripts must be evaluated with participation of foreign reviewers/editors.
- **Ethics**: must follow COPE's core practices and SciELO's own ethics guide; requires the CRediT taxonomy (or equivalent) for author contribution statements, conflict-of-interest disclosure, clinical trial registration (where applicable), biological material/DNA sequence deposit (where applicable), similarity/plagiarism checking (SciELO brokers access to Crossref's Similarity Check via Turnitin), and documented errata/retraction procedures.
- **Publication flow**: continuous publication is required for new entrants (existing journals had to adopt it by 2023). **No more than 6 months without publication, or any embargo practice, disqualifies a journal from admission or continued indexing.** Minimum recommended articles/year varies substantially by thematic area and is recalibrated periodically from the collection's own rolling averages — check the current document rather than assuming a fixed number.
- **Structure**: full texts must be XML-structured per the SciELO Publishing Schema (JATS-compliant); mandatory English-language metadata (title/abstract/keywords) even when the article's full text isn't in English; a thematic-area-dependent minimum share of full articles in English is recommended (this varies widely by field and is recalibrated periodically).
- **Required indexing**: journals must be indexed in DOAJ (journal + article metadata) and register DOIs via Crossref; citations received are tracked through bibliometric databases as part of ongoing performance review.
- **Historical note**: from 1997–2001, journals already indexed in JCR/ISI, MEDLINE/Index Medicus, or PsycInfo, or rated highly by FAPESP/CNPq evaluations, were automatically admitted. Since 2001, admission has been exclusively by Comitê Consultivo decision against the criteria above — this historical fast-track no longer applies.

## Scopus (Elsevier)

Created November 2004. ~24,039 journals indexed (snapshot from the source slides — verify current count if precision matters).

### Minimum criteria (all mandatory)
1. Peer-reviewed content, with the review process publicly described.
2. Published regularly, with an ISSN.
3. Content relevant and readable to an international audience — minimum bar: references in Roman script, abstracts and article titles in English.
4. At least 2 years of publication history.
5. A code of ethics covering malpractice prevention — **mandatory; no journal is indexed without one.** Should state responsibilities/rights of editors, reviewers, authors, and consequences for detected misconduct. Should cover: authorship/collaboration, originality, plagiarism (including self-plagiarism), fraud, conflicts of interest, and publication of retractions/corrections/errata.
   - Resources: Elsevier's Publishing Ethics Resource Kit (elsevier.com/editors/perk), COPE (publicationethics.org).

### Weighted evaluation (14 criteria across 5 categories, reviewed by the Content Selection & Advisory Board — CSAB, ~15 independent international scientists/editors covering all fields)

| Category | Weight | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| Journal Policy | 25% | Convincing editorial concept/policy; type of peer review; geographic diversity of editors; geographic diversity of authors |
| Quality of Content | 20% | Academic contribution to the field; clarity of abstracts; quality/conformity with stated aims & scope; readability of articles |
| Journal Standing | 35% | Citedness of journal's articles already in Scopus; editor standing (citation-based reputation) |
| Regularity | 10% | No delay in the publication schedule |
| Online Availability | 10% | Content available online; English-language homepage; quality of homepage |

Peer-review red flags the CSAB watches for: single main-editor-only review, suspiciously fast turnaround (<2 weeks) with "guaranteed publication," or a new journal rapidly ramping up volume.

Process funnel (2011–2019 figures from the source slides, illustrative of how selective this is): ~3,500 title suggestions/year on average → 39% meet Scopus's minimum criteria → 46% of those are accepted after CSAB review → ~630 titles/year end up meeting the full criteria. Across 2011–2019 YTD, 12,005 titles were reviewed and 5,710 (48%) accepted overall, with wide variation by country.

### Useful search technique
Scopus advanced search supports cited-reference queries even for journals not yet indexed — useful to gauge a journal's existing citation footprint before applying, or to check standing for journals already in ESCI-tier indices:
- `REFAUTH` — author of a cited reference
- `REFPUBYEAR` — year of the cited reference
- `REFSRCTITLE` — source title of the cited reference (e.g., `REFSRCTITLE("revista subjetividades")`)
- `REFTITLE` — title of the cited document

### Submission
- Self-check first: https://www.readyforscopus.com/
- Official submission: https://suggestor.step.scopus.com/suggestTitle/step1.cfm
- Track progress: https://suggestor.step.scopus.com/progressTracker

## Web of Science (Clarivate)

### Core Collection indices
- Science Citation Index Expanded (SCIE) — since 1961
- Social Science Citation Index (SSCI) — since 1973
- Arts & Humanities Citation Index (A&HCI) — since 1978
- Emerging Sources Citation Index (ESCI) — since 2015
- Conference Proceedings Citation Index (CPCI), Book Citation Index (BKCI)

Only SCIE, SSCI, and A&HCI carry a Journal Impact Factor (Journal Citation Reports, since 1976) and are considered the higher-prestige tier; ESCI/CPCI/BKCI are part of the Core Collection but don't carry a JIF. A journal can move from ESCI up into SCIE/SSCI/A&HCI ("gain impact") or get dropped back down ("lose impact") on reassessment.

Snapshot coverage (2020 JCR figures, illustrative — verify current numbers for anything precision-sensitive):
- SCIE: 9,370 journals total (111 Brazilian)
- SSCI: 3,485 journals total (19 Brazilian)
- A&HCI: 1,843 journals total (14 Brazilian)
- ESCI: 7,727 journals total (286 Brazilian)
- SCIE/SSCI/A&HCI acceptance rate: roughly 10–12%

### Evaluation funnel: 3 stages, plus a 4th for promotion beyond ESCI
1. **Initial Triage** — ISSN, journal title, publisher, URL, content access, presence of a peer-review policy, contact details.
2. **Editorial Triage** — scholarly content; titles/abstracts in English; bibliographic info in Roman script; clarity of language; timeliness/publication volume; website functionality; presence of an ethics statement; editorial and author affiliation details.
3. **Editorial Evaluation** — editorial board composition; validity of statements; evidence of peer review; content relevance; grant support details; adherence to community standards; author geographic distribution; appropriate citations to the literature.

A journal passing all three stages is admitted to **ESCI**. To go further — into SCIE/SSCI/A&HCI — it also needs to clear:

4. **Impact Evaluation** (4 criteria):
   - *Comparative Citation Analysis* — citation count and citation sources, compared against other journals in the same discipline; citations from SCIE/SSCI/A&HCI count more, but ESCI/book/proceedings citations also count.
   - *Author Citation Analysis* — authors' field reputation via their own citation record; most authors should have a WoS publication history; citation networks appropriate to the category.
   - *Editorial Board Citation Analysis* — same logic applied to the editorial board.
   - *Content Importance* — interest/importance/value to WoS users: original/unique specialization, new perspective, regional focus, or content that broadens WoS's coverage. Not reflected solely in citation activity.

Failing any stage means resubmitting after fixing the flagged issues (Initial/Editorial Triage) or a **2+ year embargo** before resubmission (failing Editorial or Impact Evaluation). A journal that clears Triage + Editorial Evaluation but not Impact Evaluation typically stays at ESCI rather than advancing.

### Useful search technique
Web of Science's "Pesquisa de referência citada" (Cited Reference Search) finds articles citing a given work or journal even when that journal isn't indexed — useful both pre-submission and for ESCI journals checking their case for promotion. Search variants of how the journal might be cited (full name, abbreviations, acronyms) and add them all before running the query.

### Submission
https://mjl.clarivate.com/wos-journal-submission/wos-journal-submission.html — gather all required information before starting; status updates come by email.

## PubMed / MEDLINE (U.S. National Library of Medicine)

**The single most important thing to get right here: "indexed in PubMed" and "MEDLINE-indexed" are not the same claim, and journals routinely advertise the weaker one as if it were the stronger one.**

- **PubMed** is a search interface, not a selective index by itself. It surfaces citations from several different sources: MEDLINE-indexed journals, **PubMed Central (PMC)** full-text content, and some additional material (e.g., NIH-funded author manuscripts deposited under funder mandates, books, certain preprints). A journal — or even just individual articles from a journal — can show up in PubMed search results via PMC or an NIH deposit mandate **without the journal ever having been selected for MEDLINE.**
- **MEDLINE** is the actual selectively-curated subset, and *that* selection is the real quality signal. As of a September 2025 NLM process change, the journal-level **Literature Selection Technical Review Committee (LSTRC)** — the external advisory committee that used to make these recommendations — was terminated. Selection is now done via direct review by **at least two external expert consultants per journal** (typically one doctoral-level scientist/physician and one medical librarian), who review roughly the last two years of the journal's content; NLM's Director makes the final call, informed by NLM Board of Regents policy and the Collection Development Guidelines. **Verify current process details on nlm.nih.gov/medline if precision matters — this changed recently and may change again.**
- **PMC (PubMed Central)** is a separate full-text archive with its own scientific/editorial quality review, generally evaluated holistically rather than against a fixed checklist. A journal already fully MEDLINE-indexed typically doesn't need a separate PMC scientific review — but the reverse isn't automatic: passing PMC review doesn't mean a journal has been, or will be, MEDLINE-indexed. Failing PMC's quality bar also makes a journal ineligible for MEDLINE review in the near term.

### MEDLINE's current scientific/editorial quality assessment — 5 elements
1. **Scope and Coverage** — clearly stated aims/scope; does authorship/editorial board/content actually match the stated scope; does it cover an under-represented research area or geography.
2. **Editorial Policies and Processes** — explicit, detailed peer-review description (type, typical number of reviewers); a policy for handling manuscripts authored by editors/board members; findable and current ethics policies; ethics-approval and informed-consent statements present where relevant; consistent conflict-of-interest/funding disclosure; full names, affiliations, and credentials for all editors/board members; sufficient diversity in the editorial board and in authorship; support for transparency, reproducibility, open science, and data sharing.
3. **Scientific (or Methodological) Rigor** — accurate abstracts; clearly stated, logical aims; reproducible methodological detail; consistency with relevant reporting guidelines (CONSORT for trials, PRISMA for systematic reviews, ARRIVE for animal studies, CARE for case reports, etc.); conclusions actually supported by the data; critical, comprehensive discussion; appropriately cited, up-to-date references.
4. **Production and Administration** — adherence to the stated publication schedule; sustainable frequency/volume; well-constructed, high-resolution figures/tables; clear writing; consistent article structure; structured abstracts for research articles/systematic reviews/meta-analyses; evidence of real editorial attention (no mislabeled figures/sections); a functional, navigable website; individual article downloads; multiple available formats.
5. **Impact** — the journal's overall significance to the field; whether it critically synthesizes and organizes knowledge rather than just publishing isolated findings.

A journal must first be judged suitable for the NLM Collection and have appropriate subject matter before this review even begins.

### Practical implication for this skill
When checking whether a paper's venue is credible, "I found it on PubMed" is not, by itself, sufficient evidence — check specifically whether the *journal* (not just the article) is MEDLINE-indexed, e.g. via NLM's catalog/journal list, rather than treating any PubMed hit as equivalent. This is exactly the kind of distinction worth surfacing explicitly rather than collapsing into a single "indexed, looks fine" verdict.

## Cross-cutting advice (applies to Redalyc, SciELO, Scopus, Web of Science, and MEDLINE)
- Self-assess honestly against the mandatory criteria before submitting — embargoes for failing mandatory items can run 6 months (Redalyc) to 2+ years (Scopus/WoS), so it's often better to delay submission and fix gaps first.
- For Scopus and WoS specifically, check existing citations to the journal via cited-reference search before applying — it's a concrete, checkable signal of standing.
- Match indexer choice to the journal's actual editorial identity and audience — a journal that genuinely serves a regional audience with a regional editorial board shouldn't necessarily chase indices built around international diversity criteria; it's a fit question, not a strict hierarchy.
- Criteria evolve over time and involve some reviewer judgment — treat every specific numeric criterion in this file as accurate as of its own section's source date (noted at the top of this file), not a permanently fixed rule. MEDLINE's process in particular changed in September 2025 and may change again. If a decision is high-stakes, verify the current criteria directly on the indexer's own site before relying on this file.
